# Touch and Gestures

> Direct physical manipulation of on-screen objects through motions like tap, swipe, drag, and pinch.

**When to use it:** Touchscreen and hybrid devices — tap to activate, swipe to dismiss or reveal actions, drag to reorder, pinch to zoom; reserve custom gestures for specialized, frequent tasks.

**Guidelines**
- Match expectations for standard gestures: tap selects or activates, swipe scrolls or reveals, drag moves, long-press shows more options.
- Don't repurpose a familiar gesture for an app-unique action, or invent a new one for something users expect a standard gesture to do.
- Respond with immediate, continuous feedback so users can predict a gesture's outcome partway through the motion.
- Make every gesture-driven action also reachable a simpler way, such as a visible control; gestures should supplement, not replace.
- Treat custom gestures as discoverable, easy, and distinct, and teach them in context — never as the only path to an important action.
- Keep scrolling and other primary motions smooth and predictable.
- Indicate clearly when a gesture isn't available so the interface doesn't feel frozen.

**Accessibility**
- Provide non-gesture alternatives for users who can't swipe or pinch; never gate functionality behind a single gesture.
- Keep tap targets large enough and well-spaced for imprecise touch.
- Don't disable the system's zoom — many users rely on it to read.
- Offer equivalents reachable by keyboard and assistive tech for any drag, swipe, or reorder action.

**Avoid**
- Hijacking native gestures such as edge-swipe navigation, pull-to-refresh, pinch-to-zoom, or two-finger scroll.
- Making a custom, multi-finger, or hard-to-describe gesture the only way to do something.
- Gestures that give no visible feedback and no hint that they exist.
