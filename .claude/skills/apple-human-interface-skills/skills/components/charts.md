# Charts

> A chart organizes data visually to highlight key insights and help people understand and decide.

**When to use it:** To compare values, show change over time, or reveal trends, relationships, and outliers — bars for categories, lines for time, points for relationships.

**Guidelines**
- Choose the mark type from the message you want to convey; combine types when it clarifies.
- Use a fixed axis range when the minimum and maximum are meaningful, and a dynamic range when values vary widely.
- Choose the lower bound deliberately — zero suits bar comparisons but can hide differences far from zero.
- Use familiar tick sequences and only as many grid lines as needed.
- Keep the data the most prominent layer; give the chart a title that summarizes its main message.
- Let people scrub or hover for exact values, but never require interaction to reveal critical information.

**Accessibility**
- Provide a text summary of the chart type, axes, bounds, and main takeaway, plus labels per mark or group.
- Don't rely on color alone — use shape, pattern, or direct labels, and add separation between adjacent colors.
- Let people move through the data in a logical order, not just see it.
- Convey important changes without depending on animation alone, respecting reduced motion.

**Avoid**
- Conveying essential information through color alone.
- Cluttering the chart with excessive grid lines or labels.
- Hiding key information behind required interactions.
